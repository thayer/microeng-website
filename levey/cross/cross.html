Integrated Machines & Small Parts

R&D Panel discussion material for Marion Cross 6th grade class
Chris Levey, Dartmouth Thayer School Microengineering Laboratory

We've learned well how to scale down electronics.
  • Advantage due to small size itself (laser pointers, LEDs, hearing aids)

  • Advantage due to large number of them (transistors in a computer)
What does the future hold?
  • Smaller yet electronics (integrated circuits)

  • Making other things small--MicroElectroMechanical Systems (MEMS). (Here is a MEMS chip that was shown on an X-files episode to represent an alien chip, but this chip was really made and many of the devices on it work).

    Systems smaller than a hair diameter (some such machines in each category have been made by Dartmouth students):

    • Mechanical parts (moving parts, gears, motors, pushers, accelerometers
    • Fluidic parts (amplifiers, fluidic circuits, pressure sensors, pumps, valves, flow controllers)
    • Optical parts (lens correctors, mirrors, ...)
    • Parts that interface with biological systems (close-up)
How is it done?
  • Surface Micromachining--Photolithography
  • Cleanrooms needed when devices are smaller than dust...
Smaller yet?
  • Writing with single Atoms (Xenon)
  • Stadium (Iron atoms on Copper; electron waves)
  • Molecular wires (fundamental research).
  • More like science fiction at this time: atomic bearings to be used in robots constructed an atom at a time.
