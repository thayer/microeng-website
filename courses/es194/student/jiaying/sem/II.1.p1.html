Direct Evaporation

also named as Vacuum Evaporation, the materials are vaporized and deposited in the absence of any plasma intentionally generated.

Schematic of a vacuum evaporation system

  • Type of coating materials: usually pure metal, some alloys, and compounds
  • Kinetic energy : 0.1 to 1 eV
  • Deposition pressure : 10^-3 to 10^-6 Pa (10^-5 to 10^-8 torr)
  • Substrate temperature : 200 to 1600 C
  • Coating thickness range : 0.1 to 100 E-6m
  • Deposition rate : 1 to 25 E-6m/min
  • Relative adhesion : Fair to good at elevated temperature
  • Substrate may be floating, grounded, or electrically biased
  • Substrate heated for a good adhesion and microstructure

Evaporation of alloys and compounds

Advantages:

  • Simple, cheap
  • Extremely high deposition rates
  • No gas scattering

Disadvantages:

  • Low kinetic energy, inadequate adhesion
  • High substrate temperature for dense coatings
  • Imprecise control of stoichiometry of compound coatings
  • Substrate must generally be rotated for uniform coating

Applications:

    used extensivly in optical and decorative applications.
